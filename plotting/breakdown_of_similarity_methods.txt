Here is a breakdown of the similarity metrics, as a brief overview from Claude

Jensen-Shannon divergence measures how different two probability distributions are. In the BoW context, each group (e.g. patient_important, non_important) is treated as a probability distribution over n-gram strings — so the frequency of each n-gram like "ST-CQ" is one dimension of that distribution. JS divergence computes a smoothed, symmetric version of KL divergence: it takes the average of the two distributions as a midpoint M, then measures how far each distribution is from M using KL divergence, and averages those two numbers. The result is bounded between 0 (identical distributions) and 1 (completely non-overlapping). In practice a score near 0 means the two groups use n-gram patterns in very similar proportions; a score near 1 means they use almost entirely different patterns. Because it operates on the full n-gram distribution it captures both which patterns appear and how frequently, but it gives you one number per group-pair per n-gram size — it doesn't tell you which specific n-grams drove the divergence. That's what the TF-IDF and dominant n-gram outputs are for.

NetLSD symmetrises the adjacency matrix of the directed transition graph (treating ST→Q and Q→ST as the same edge with combined weight) then computes the eigenvalues of the normalised Laplacian. The heat trace at time t is the sum of exp(−t·λᵢ) over all eigenvalues — physically this is how much "heat" remains in the graph if you released one unit at every node and let it diffuse for time t. At small t the heat trace reflects local structure like node degrees and small cycles; at large t it reflects global structure like how many connected components there are and overall path lengths. The L2 distance between two graphs' heat trace curves is the NetLSD distance. Because the adjacency is symmetrised, NetLSD is completely blind to edge direction — it cannot distinguish a graph where statements mostly lead to questions from one where questions mostly lead to statements. It is useful for comparing undirected structural topology but should not be trusted for anything involving conversational flow direction.

Magnetic Laplacian keeps the directional information by encoding each edge's net asymmetry as a complex phase. For a pair of nodes u and v, the symmetric weight is (w_uv + w_vu)/2 as before, but the adjacency entry is multiplied by exp(i·2π·q·s) where s is the net directionality — +1 if traffic flows purely u→v, −1 if purely v→u, 0 if balanced. The parameter q=0.25 (quarter turn) gives the phase full sensitivity to direction. The resulting matrix is Hermitian so its eigenvalues are real, and the heat trace is computed the same way as NetLSD. Two graphs that look identical after symmetrisation but have opposite dominant directions will produce different eigenspectra and therefore different distances. The weakness is phase cancellation — if a graph has many edges with opposite directions that happen to cancel in the eigenspectrum, two structurally different graphs can appear similar. This is why the Hashimoto method was added alongside it.

Hashimoto (non-backtracking) operator works completely differently from the Laplacian-based methods. Its nodes are the directed edges of the graph (not the DA groups), and its entries encode which directed edge can follow which other directed edge without immediately reversing. So the entry B[(u→v), (v→w)] = weight of v→w, for any w≠u. The "no backtracking" constraint (w≠u) is what makes it inherently directional — reversals simply do not exist in the operator. The symmetrised version (B + Bᵀ)/2 gives real eigenvalues cheaply while still preserving much of the directional structure. The heat trace is then normalised by edge count (since the operator's dimension is |E|×|E| rather than |N|×|N|). Because it is defined on edges rather than nodes, it captures longer-range flow patterns that the node-level Laplacian misses — two graphs can have identical node degree distributions (identical NetLSD) but very different edge-to-edge transition structure (different Hashimoto). It has no free parameters, which is an advantage over the magnetic Laplacian's q. The main practical limitation is that its matrix grows as |E|², so for very dense raw-granularity graphs it becomes slow.
